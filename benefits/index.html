Benefits Completeness
Advisor assessment

A competitive nonprofit benefits package at the 50th percentile should include: medical (employer covering 75–85% of employee premium), dental, vision, a retirement plan with some employer contribution, basic life insurance, and an EAP.

The hidden cost of a thin benefits package is always greater than the cost of improving it. Benefits are the primary tool for narrowing the total compensation gap between nonprofit and private sector.

COBRA Administration

△Federal COBRA applies to employers with 20 or more employees who sponsor group health plans. Texas has a state continuation law (Texas Insurance Code Chapter 1251) that applies to employers with 2–19 employees offering group health coverage, providing 9 months of continuation at the group rate. ERISA Obligations
The Employee Retirement Income Security Act governs most employer-sponsored benefit plans. These obligations apply regardless of employer size.
⚖ERISA does not cover government or church plans. It applies to virtually all private-sector employers — including nonprofits — that sponsor retirement, health, or welfare benefit plans. Noncompliance is not a gray area: ERISA carries civil penalties, excise taxes, and personal fiduciary liability for plan administrators.

Mental Health Parity — MHPAEA
⚖The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA) requires that group health plans covering mental health or substance use disorder (MH/SUD) benefits apply no more restrictive financial requirements or treatment limitations than those applied to medical/surgical benefits. This applies to employers with 50 or more employees. Enforcement has increased significantly since 2021 under the Consolidated Appropriations Act, which added a comparative analysis requirement.
RequirementWho It Applies ToWhat It Means in Practice
Parity in financial requirementsALEs (50+ employees) offering group health plans covering MH/SUDCopays, deductibles, and out-of-pocket maximums for mental health visits cannot be more restrictive than for comparable medical visits. Separate, higher mental health deductibles are a common violation.
Parity in treatment limitationsSameVisit limits, prior authorization requirements, and fail-first protocols for MH/SUD cannot be more restrictive than for analogous medical/surgical benefits. Unlimited medical visits paired with a 30-visit mental health cap is a common violation.
Non-quantitative treatment limitation (NQTL) comparative analysisSame — required since December 2021 (CAA)Plans must perform and document a comparative analysis demonstrating that NQTLs (prior auth, step therapy, network standards) are applied no more stringently to MH/SUD than to medical/surgical benefits. This document must be produced to DOL or participants upon request.
Annual reporting (CAA 2023)SamePlans must submit an attestation of MHPAEA compliance with their Form 5500 (when applicable) and provide the comparative analysis to participants upon request within 30 days.

Section 125 Cafeteria Plan
Pre-tax premium elections reduce employee taxable income and employer FICA — one of the most accessible tools for small nonprofits.
ℹA Section 125 (Cafeteria) Plan allows employees to pay their share of benefit premiums with pre-tax dollars, reducing their taxable income and the employer's FICA obligation. It is one of the most valuable and underutilized tools available to small nonprofits. An employer with 30 employees paying $300/month in health premiums saves approximately $2,750/year in employer FICA alone by implementing a Section 125 plan — before counting the value to employees.

Benefits Strategy & Sector Benchmarks
What organizations at this size and budget should be offering, what the competition for talent looks like, and how to make the case for benefit investment to a skeptical board.
ℹBenefits are not a compliance obligation — they are a talent strategy. An organization that pays at the 50th percentile of market salary but provides a strong benefits package can be more competitive in total compensation than one paying at the 65th percentile with thin benefits. The gap between what nonprofits offer and what employees can get elsewhere is rarely just about salary.

Making the Case to the Board
The cost of turnover argument
Replacing a professional-level employee costs 1–2× their annual salary when recruiting, onboarding, and lost productivity are included. A single regrettable departure at a $60,000 salary costs the organization $60,000–$120,000. Improving the benefits package by $2,400/year per employee for all 20 employees costs $48,000 — less than one replacement. The math is not close.
The compensation gap argument
The nonprofit salary gap relative to private sector is real. But total compensation — which includes benefits — can be more competitive than salary alone suggests. An organization that pays $65,000 with exceptional health coverage, a 5% retirement match, and 25 days PTO may be more competitive in total compensation than a private employer paying $72,000 with cost-sharing on benefits and 15 days PTO.
The funder argument
Many funders have shifted away from the nonprofit starvation cycle narrative. Grantors including Ford, MacArthur, and most community foundations now recognize that underpaying staff is a program risk, not a fiscal virtue. Presenting a benefits investment to a funder as a talent retention and organizational sustainability strategy is increasingly well-received — particularly when framed around mission continuity and institutional knowledge.
