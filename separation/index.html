Voluntary Resignation
Process and documentation for employee-initiated separations.
ℹTexas does not require employees to give notice. If the employee gives 2 weeks notice and you ask them to leave immediately, the separation becomes employer-initiated and final pay rules change — you must pay within 6 calendar days rather than at the next regular payday. Consider this before sending someone home early after they resign.
Involuntary Termination
Documentation and process for employer-initiated separations.
⚖Before terminating any employee, review the termination decision for consistency with prior practice, protected class risk, and whether the reason is documented. The conversation confirming termination should be brief, direct, and not argumentative. The decision has been made — the meeting is to communicate it, not debate it.
△For employees age 40 or older, the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA) governs any severance agreement that includes a waiver of ADEA (age discrimination) claims. Waivers must be written in plain language, reference the ADEA specifically, advise consulting counsel, provide 21 days to consider and 7 days to revoke. OWBPA waivers must be reviewed by employment counsel.
Reduction in Force (RIF)
Layoff planning, selection criteria documentation, and WARN Act considerations.
△A RIF requires careful pre-planning to minimize legal exposure. The selection criteria must be documented before decisions are made. Once the list is built, conduct an adverse impact analysis — if the RIF disproportionately affects employees in a protected class, that is a significant legal risk regardless of whether the intent was discriminatory.
ConsiderationDetail
WARN Act (Federal)Applies to employers with 100+ employees. Requires 60 days advance notice for plant closings or mass layoffs (50+ employees at a single site, or 33% of workforce). Failure to provide notice results in 60 days back pay and benefits.
Texas Mini-WARNTexas does not have a state WARN Act. Federal WARN applies.
Selection CriteriaMust be documented before selection. Common criteria: skills inventory match to future needs, performance ratings, seniority, position elimination. Cannot use subjective factors without documented basis.
Adverse Impact AnalysisBefore finalizing the RIF list, analyze the demographics of those selected vs. the workforce. If the RIF has a disparate impact on a protected group, review and document the business justification before proceeding.
OWBPA (40+ employees)For group separations involving employees 40+, additional notice requirements apply including 45 days to consider a severance agreement and information about the class of employees selected and not selected.

Reference Policy Framework
What the organization says — and does not say — when contacted about a former employee.
⚖Reference practices are one of the most legally underestimated areas of HR. Providing negative references that cannot be substantiated, or inconsistently providing references for some employees and not others, creates defamation and discrimination exposure. A clear, consistent reference policy protects the organization and creates a defensible practice.
Standard Reference Policy (Recommended)
All reference requests for former employees should be directed to HR. The organization's standard practice is to confirm: (1) dates of employment, (2) position(s) held, and (3) whether the individual is eligible for rehire. HR does not provide information beyond these three elements unless the former employee has provided written authorization for expanded disclosure.
Why This Policy Protects the Organization
RiskHow the Policy Addresses It
DefamationBy limiting disclosure to verifiable factual information (dates, title, rehire status), the organization eliminates the primary basis for defamation claims — statements of opinion or unverified negative characterizations.
DiscriminationApplying the same policy to all former employees regardless of race, gender, age, disability, or the reason for separation prevents discriminatory reference practices — including giving positive verbal references for some employees but not others.
InconsistencyWhen managers give informal references without authorization, the organization loses control of what is said. A no-exception routing policy closes this gap.
Negligent referralIf the organization provides a positive reference for a former employee who had documented safety or conduct issues, and the new employer or their clients are harmed, the organization may face negligent referral liability. The standard policy creates a firewall.
When an Employee Requests Expanded References
If a former employee requests a more detailed reference, require a written authorization form signed by the employee specifying: (1) who may be contacted, (2) what information may be shared, and (3) the duration of the authorization. Keep the signed form on file. Even with authorization, limit statements to factual information that can be substantiated by documentation.
Rehire Eligibility Designation
DesignationMeaningWhen to Use
Eligible for RehireNo documented performance, conduct, or integrity concerns that would preclude rehiringVoluntary resignations in good standing; ends of contracts; RIF separations without cause
Eligible for Rehire with ReviewPrior employment issues that would warrant evaluation before any rehire decision is madePerformance concerns that were documented but not sufficient for termination; minor policy violations
Not Eligible for RehireDocumented conduct, integrity, or performance issues that would preclude rehiringTerminations for cause, policy violations, misconduct, or dishonesty. Must be documented and consistent — not applied inconsistently across protected classes.
Unemployment Insurance Response
How to respond to Texas Workforce Commission claims and protect the organization's unemployment insurance rate.
ℹTexas employers pay unemployment insurance taxes based on their experience rating — the history of claims paid against their account. Every claim paid increases the employer's UI tax rate for subsequent years. Responding promptly and accurately to TWC claims is both a financial and a legal obligation.
The TWC Claims Process
StepTimelineWhat the Employer Must Do
Initial Claim FiledFormer employee files after separationNo immediate action required; wait for TWC notice
TWC Claim Notice Sent to EmployerWithin days of claim filingRespond within the deadline stated on the notice — typically 14 days. Missing this deadline means the claim will likely be approved by default regardless of merit.
Employer ResponseWithin the notice deadlineProvide: reason for separation in the employer's own words, all relevant facts, and copies of any documentation (warnings, PIP, investigation report, attendance records). Be factual and objective — not emotional.
TWC DeterminationAfter reviewing both sidesIf the determination is adverse, the employer has 14 days to appeal. An appeal hearing before a TWC hearing officer follows.
Appeal HearingTypically 4–8 weeks after filingPrepare documentation and, if applicable, witnesses. Hearings are conducted by phone. Legal representation is permitted but not required. Documentation is the primary evidence.
Grounds for Contesting a Claim
Separation ReasonEligible for Benefits?Employer Response Strategy
Voluntary resignation — employee's choiceGenerally no, unless employee left for "good cause connected to the work"Document that the resignation was voluntary and uncoerced. Provide any resignation letter or email. If employee claims constructive discharge, prepare to rebut.
Termination for misconductNo — misconduct disqualifiesProvide specific, documented facts. TWC defines misconduct as a deliberate violation of the employer's reasonable rules or standards. Poor performance alone is generally not misconduct — distinguish clearly.
Termination for poor performanceGenerally yes — performance is not misconductThe employer may still respond to provide the record, but contesting a pure performance termination is usually not successful and not recommended unless there is a misconduct element.
Reduction in Force / Position eliminatedYes — no fault of employeeDo not contest. Respond confirming the RIF, stating the business reason, and noting that the employee performed satisfactorily. Contesting a legitimate RIF claim damages employee relations and rarely succeeds.
End of contract / temporary assignmentYes — lack of workRespond confirming the contract end date and nature of the assignment. Do not contest if the end was predetermined and unrelated to conduct.
△The most common employer mistake in unemployment proceedings is providing opinions, characterizations, or undocumented allegations in response to a TWC claim. The TWC hearing officer has seen every version of "they were a bad employee." What they respond to is documentation. If the conduct was documented, the employer will likely prevail. If it was not, the claim will likely be approved — and that is the correct outcome.
Separation Log
Cumulative record of all employee separations. Each entry is a separate record. Retain for 7 years post-separation.
ℹA complete separation log supports adverse impact analysis, unemployment defense, and pattern identification. Consistent documentation across all separations — not just terminations for cause — protects the organization. If only certain types of separations are documented, the inconsistency itself becomes a liability.
