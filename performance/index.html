Goal Framework
Annual Review Timeline — Sector Best Practice
ℹA consistent, transparent review timeline builds trust and reduces the performance conversation from an event employees dread to a rhythm they can prepare for. Communicate the timeline to all employees at the start of the review period. Surprises in the review room are almost always a management failure.
PhaseTimingWho ActsBest Practice Note
Goal SettingFirst 2 weeks of each review periodEmployee drafts; manager approvesGoals should be co-created, not handed down. Employee ownership of goals correlates directly with performance outcomes.
Mid-Cycle Check-InMidpoint of review periodManager and employeeNot optional. Surprises in the formal review that were not raised at mid-year are a manager failure — not a performance failure. Use this to course-correct, recognize, and recalibrate.
Employee Self-Assessment2 weeks before manager review deadlineEmployeeSelf-assessment should precede manager review — not follow it. When employees write first, managers get insight, not validation of their own pre-formed view.
Manager Review Completed1 week before review meetingManager; reviewed by HRHR review before delivery catches discriminatory language, unsupported ratings, and consistency issues across the team. This is not bureaucracy — it is risk management.
Performance Review MeetingScheduled review windowManager and employee; HR availableThe meeting is a conversation, not a reading of the document. Employee should be given the written review before the meeting so they can prepare a response. Ambush reviews are bad practice.
Review Finalized and FiledWithin 1 week of meetingManager; HRSignatures are required — employee signature acknowledges receipt, not agreement. If employee refuses to sign, note refusal with a witness. File in personnel record.
Compensation DecisionsFollowing review finalizationManager; HR; FinanceSeparate the performance conversation from the compensation conversation when possible. When combined, employees focus on pay and stop hearing feedback.
Performance Review Log
A cumulative record of formal performance reviews, mid-cycle check-ins, and ratings. Each entry is a separate record — the log builds over time and across employees.
ℹThis log is the organization's official record of formal performance activity. It should include annual and mid-year reviews, any formal check-ins with documented outcomes, and records of ratings assigned. Consistent documentation across the team reduces exposure to disparate treatment claims.

Documentation Standards
What to document, how to write it, and what belongs in the personnel file.
ℹThe single most common HR failure in employment litigation is inadequate documentation. Undocumented performance conversations never happened. Verbal warnings that weren't written down don't exist. Document in real time — not retroactively.
Progressive Discipline Framework
△Progressive discipline is appropriate for performance and minor conduct issues. Serious misconduct — harassment, theft, violence, fraud, safety violations — may warrant immediate termination without any prior steps. Document why you skipped steps when you do. A policy that says "we always give three warnings" is not a policy — it is a liability.
StepWhen to UseWhat Must Be DocumentedHow It Should Be Delivered
1. Coaching ConversationFirst observation of a performance or conduct issue. Informal. No formal record required — though a brief manager's note is best practice.Date, issue discussed, expectations stated, employee response. Email to self is sufficient.Private 1:1. Conversational. Not threatening. "I want to give you feedback before this becomes a bigger issue."
2. Verbal Warning (Documented)Issue continues after coaching, or initial severity warrants more formal action. Written record of what was said verbally.Date, specific behavior, policy or expectation violated, expected change, timeframe for improvement, employee response, manager signature.Private meeting. Serious tone. "This is a formal verbal warning." Give employee a copy of the written record.
3. Written WarningIssue continues after verbal warning, or initial severity warrants skipping step 2. Formal disciplinary document.All elements of verbal warning plus: reference to any prior discipline, clear statement of consequences if behavior continues, employee acknowledgment of receipt.Private meeting with HR present when possible. Give employee a copy. If employee refuses to sign, note the refusal on the document with a witness.
4. Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)Performance deficiency has not improved despite prior warnings, or role-specific performance failure requiring structured intervention. See PIP tab.Concerns, requirements, support, timeline, consequences, dual signatures.Formal meeting. Manager + HR. Present as an opportunity, not a prelude to termination — even when it is. Document the meeting itself.
5. SeparationPerformance has not improved through PIP, or conduct is serious enough to warrant termination. Always review before acting.Complete documentation trail through all prior steps. Confirmation that termination was reviewed for consistency and protected class risk before action was taken.Brief, direct, not argumentative. Decision is final. See Tool 12 — Separation.
ADA and FMLA Intersection with Performance
⚠This is one of the highest-risk scenarios in employment law. When performance issues coincide with a medical condition, disability, or leave — the legal landscape changes dramatically. Do not proceed with discipline or termination in this scenario without HR review and, in serious cases, employment counsel.
ScenarioWhat the Law RequiresWhat Many Employers Get Wrong
Employee on FMLA has unmet performance expectationsFMLA leave cannot be counted as an absence against the employee. Performance standards that were unmet while on leave should generally be paused. You may hold the employee to the same standards when they return — but you cannot discipline for performance that occurred during protected leave.Issuing discipline that includes FMLA absences in the attendance record, or failing to distinguish between pre-leave and during-leave performance issues.
Employee requests accommodation after discipline beginsThe accommodation request triggers an obligation to engage in the interactive process — even if discipline is already underway. Continuing discipline without addressing the accommodation request is a retaliation risk.Treating the accommodation request as irrelevant to an ongoing disciplinary process, or fast-tracking termination after an accommodation request is made.
Employee's performance issue may be disability-relatedIf the employer has reason to know that a performance issue may be related to a disability, the ADA requires consideration of whether a reasonable accommodation would enable adequate performance before proceeding to termination.Terminating an employee for performance without ever considering whether the conduct or deficiency was connected to a disability or whether accommodation was possible.
Employee cannot perform essential functions due to disabilityIf an employee genuinely cannot perform the essential functions of the role even with accommodation, termination may be lawful. But the employer must have engaged in a good-faith interactive process first and documented why no reasonable accommodation was possible.Skipping the interactive process and going straight to termination, leaving the organization exposed to an ADA failure-to-accommodate claim.
Performance Conversation Templates
Frameworks for common performance conversations. These are starting points — adapt to the person and situation. The language matters as much as the structure.
Mid-Cycle Check-In
"I want to use our time today to make sure we are both clear on how things are going and where to focus the rest of the year. Before I share my perspective — what are you most proud of from the last few months? And where do you feel least clear or supported?" [Listen first. Then share your observations — both strengths and areas for development. Agree on 1–2 priorities for the rest of the period. Document the conversation in a brief written summary and send it the same day.]
Recognition and Commendation
"I want to be specific about what I observed and why it mattered. [Name what they did.] That kind of [skill/quality/judgment] is not common, and it made a real difference for [the team / the client / the outcome]. I want to make sure that work gets the acknowledgment it deserves." [Document recognition in writing — even a brief email — and retain it. Pattern of positive documentation matters in both promotion decisions and if the employment relationship ever becomes contested.]
Opening a Difficult Performance Conversation
"I want to talk with you today because I have noticed [specific behavior or pattern] and I want to address it directly. I am sharing this because I want you to succeed here, and I think we need to be honest with each other about what is working and what is not. Can you help me understand what has been happening from your perspective?" [Do not soften to the point of obscuring the message. The employee must leave understanding that this is a serious concern, not a vague suggestion. Document the conversation date, what was said, and the employee response.]
Delivering a Written Warning
"I am meeting with you today to issue a formal written warning regarding [specific issue]. This is a serious matter. I want to be clear about what the concern is, what we need to see change, and what happens if it does not. I will give you a copy of this document. I want to hear your response before we close this meeting. Do you have questions about anything I have described?" [HR should be present if available. Obtain employee signature acknowledging receipt — not agreement. If refused, document the refusal with a witness. File original in personnel file immediately.]
Introducing a PIP
"We have had several conversations about [issue] and I have not seen the improvement I was hoping for. At this point, I am putting a formal performance improvement plan in place. I want to be direct with you: this is serious. At the same time, this is a structured opportunity to demonstrate that you can meet the standards of this role. I am going to walk you through every requirement. I want you to ask questions, and I want to be clear about what success looks like and what happens if we do not get there." [HR must be present. Both parties sign. Give the employee a copy. Schedule the first PIP check-in before leaving the meeting. Document the delivery meeting itself separately from the PIP document.]
Year-End Review Closing
"Before we close — I want to make sure we leave this conversation with the same understanding of where you stand, where you are going, and what you need from me. In your own words, what is your takeaway from today? And what is one thing I could do differently in how I support you this coming year?" [End every formal review by asking the employee to reflect. It closes the loop, surfaces anything that did not land as intended, and signals that this is a two-way relationship. Document the response.]
