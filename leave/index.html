FMLA Reference & Eligibility
Family and Medical Leave Act — federal law applying to employers with 50+ employees.
ℹFMLA applies to organizations with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius. Eligible employees must have worked for the employer for at least 12 months and 1,250 hours in the past 12 months. Texas has no state FMLA law that expands federal protections.
ItemDetail
EntitlementUp to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per year. Up to 26 weeks for military caregiver leave.
Qualifying ReasonsSerious health condition of employee or immediate family member; birth/adoption/foster placement of child; qualifying military exigency; military caregiver
DesignationEmployer must designate FMLA-qualifying leave as FMLA within 5 business days of having enough information — even if the employee hasn't requested it and even if the employer would prefer not to.
Notice to EmployeeProvide Designation Notice (WH-382) within 5 days. Provide Rights and Responsibilities Notice (WH-381) when leave is foreseeable or within 5 days of learning leave is needed.
Intermittent LeaveMay be taken in increments as small as one hour. Employer cannot deny. May require medical certification for intermittent leave.
Substitution of Paid LeaveEmployer may require — or employee may elect — to substitute accrued paid leave concurrently with FMLA. The substitution must run concurrently, not consecutively.
Key Employer MistakesFailing to designate qualifying leave; discouraging employees from taking FMLA; counting FMLA absences against attendance; denying reinstatement to equivalent position; failing to maintain health benefits during FMLA
Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA) — Effective June 2023
△The PWFA took effect June 27, 2023 and applies to employers with 15 or more employees. It is separate from the ADA and the Pregnancy Discrimination Act. Many employers are not yet in compliance. This is an active enforcement priority for the EEOC.
PWFA RequirementDetail
Covered ConditionKnown limitations related to, affected by, or arising out of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions — including lactation, postpartum depression, and fertility treatments
Reasonable AccommodationEmployer must provide reasonable accommodation for known limitations unless it causes undue hardship. Unlike ADA, the limitation does not need to rise to "disability" level.
Examples of Required AccommodationsMore frequent breaks, schedule adjustments, telework, temporary transfer to a less strenuous position, leave, closer parking, ability to sit, access to water, exceptions to certain uniform requirements
No Documentation Required for Minor AdjustmentsFor minor adjustments (e.g., more bathroom breaks), employer cannot require documentation of the medical need. Documentation may be required for more significant accommodations.
Interaction with ADAADA may also apply if the condition rises to disability level. Analyze both statutes. PWFA is broader — apply PWFA first, then assess ADA if the condition qualifies.
Retaliation ProhibitedEmployer may not retaliate against employees for requesting or using PWFA accommodations, opposing discriminatory practices, or participating in PWFA proceedings.

ADA Leave as Reasonable Accommodation
When leave is required as a disability accommodation under the Americans with Disabilities Act.
⚖ADA applies to employers with 15 or more employees. A finite period of leave may be a reasonable accommodation under the ADA even when FMLA does not apply or has been exhausted. The key question is whether the additional leave would cause undue hardship. This is a fact-specific analysis — document everything and consult counsel for every ADA accommodation decision involving leave.
ConceptApplication
Interactive ProcessWhen an employee requests a leave extension or accommodation, the employer must engage in a good-faith interactive dialogue. This is not optional. Failure to engage is itself an ADA violation — independent of whether the accommodation would have been granted.
Indefinite LeaveADA does not require indefinite leave. However, "indefinite" and "open-ended" are different things. A leave with no confirmed end date is not automatically unreasonable — the employer must conduct a real undue hardship analysis, not assume one.
Leave After FMLA ExhaustionWhen FMLA ends, the ADA analysis begins. The employer cannot automatically terminate an employee because FMLA leave is exhausted if the employee has a disability and additional leave would not cause undue hardship.
DocumentationMaintain a written record of all accommodation requests, the interactive process, every decision made, and the specific business rationale for any denial. The documentation trail is the legal defense.
Medical CertificationEmployer may request medical documentation sufficient to establish that the employee has a disability and that the requested accommodation is related to the limitation. Cannot require the employee's entire medical record.
Interactive Process — Step by Step
ℹThe interactive process is a legally required collaborative dialogue between employer and employee to identify an effective reasonable accommodation. Both parties must participate in good faith. The employer must initiate it when a need for accommodation is identified — even if the employee does not use the words "reasonable accommodation" or "ADA."
StepEmployer ActionDocumentation Required
1. Recognize the triggerEmployee requests leave extension, mentions a health condition affecting work, or the employer observes performance issues that may be disability-related. Any of these triggers the process.Note the date, what was said or observed, and who was present
2. Acknowledge and initiateNotify the employee in writing that the organization is initiating the interactive process. Ask the employee to describe the limitation and what they believe would help. Do not assume. Do not lead.Written notice to employee; retain copy in HR file
3. Gather informationRequest medical certification limited to the functional limitations — not the diagnosis or full medical history. Give a reasonable deadline (typically 15 business days) and extend if needed.Written request for certification; deadline documented
4. Identify accommodationsWith the employee, identify potential accommodations that address the functional limitation without eliminating the essential functions of the role. Consider all options before concluding undue hardship.Written record of options considered and rationale for acceptance or rejection
5. Implement and monitorImplement the agreed accommodation and set a follow-up date (typically 30–90 days) to assess whether it is working. Document the outcome and any adjustments made.Accommodation agreement in writing; follow-up meeting notes
6. Denial (if applicable)If accommodation is denied as an undue hardship, provide written notice with specific, documented reasons referencing the cost, disruption, or operational impact. Offer alternatives if any exist. Consult legal counsel before issuing denial.Written denial with documented undue hardship analysis; legal review notation
Undue Hardship Factors
△Undue hardship is a high bar — it means significant difficulty or expense. Cost alone is rarely sufficient for nonprofits. The DOL and EEOC have consistently found that most leave accommodations of defined duration are not undue hardships for employers with functioning operations. Document the analysis regardless of the outcome.
FactorAnalysis
Nature and cost of the accommodationWhat does it actually cost in dollars and operational disruption? For leave, the primary cost is covering the absent employee's duties. Document how long coverage has been maintained, the cost of temporary coverage, and the point at which that cost becomes unsustainable.
Overall financial resourcesThe organization's budget and financial capacity matters. A small organization with limited reserves has a stronger undue hardship argument than a large one. But even small organizations must make reasonable efforts first.
Impact on operationsWhat functions cannot be performed without this employee? Has coverage worked so far? Would extending leave meaningfully change the operational impact? If the operation has functioned for 12 weeks, it is harder to argue that 2 more weeks is an undue hardship.
Type of operation and workforceThe nature of the work and the availability of substitutes matters. A role requiring unique expertise or a small team with no backup has a stronger hardship argument than an administrative role in a larger organization.

1. Leave Philosophy
Sets the tone for every policy that follows. This language appears at the top of your policy document.
This statement will open your policy document. Mission-driven organizations often name rest and sustainability explicitly — staff notice when the org walks the talk.
2. Vacation & Paid Time Off
Sector benchmark: 15 days/year is below competitive; 20 days is the nonprofit 50th percentile; 25+ days signals a strong talent commitment. Unlimited PTO sounds generous but often results in less time taken — set a minimum use requirement if you choose this.
Include the number and whether hours or days. E.g., "20 days (160 hours)".
Best practice for unlimited PTO: set a minimum to prevent employees from feeling like they cannot take time off. Without a minimum, unlimited policies often produce less actual rest than capped policies.
Sector trend is moving toward Day 1 eligibility, particularly for senior hires. Long waiting periods disproportionately affect employees who most need flexibility.
Texas permits "use it or lose it" policies. However, a full carry-over or capped carry-over signals organizational trust. "Use it or lose it" can incentivize presenteeism — employees come in sick rather than lose days.
Critical: if your written policy states accrued PTO is paid out, it becomes a wage under Texas Payday Law. Failure to pay is a wage violation. This language must match your handbook exactly.
3. Sick Leave
Best practice: separate sick leave banks are more equitable. Employees with chronic illness or caregiving responsibilities can exhaust combined PTO banks leaving nothing for rest. Nonprofit sector standard is 7-10 days/year in a separate sick bank.
Best practice: allow sick leave for family care. Employees who cannot use sick time for an ill child must choose between losing PTO and coming to work distracted. Restricting sick leave to the employee alone is increasingly seen as a retention risk.
Requiring a note for every absence is administratively burdensome and signals distrust. Most organizations require documentation only for extended absences that may trigger FMLA review.
Allowing sick leave to carry over (often uncapped) is a best practice — it functions as a long-term disability cushion and signals that the organization values employee health as an ongoing commitment.
4. Bereavement Leave
ⓘBereavement policy is one of the most visible signals of organizational culture. How an org responds when an employee loses someone reveals what it actually values. Restrictive policies are noticed and remembered. Best practice has shifted significantly — modern bereavement policies include chosen family, pregnancy loss, and pet loss. These are not luxuries; they are signals of humanity.
Sector best practice: 5-7 days for immediate family. 3 days is below market and does not account for travel, planning, or the reality of grief. 7 days (one week) is increasingly seen as the minimum that communicates genuine care.
Best practice: include chosen family and pregnancy loss. Grief does not follow legal definitions. Employees whose partners or chosen family are excluded from bereavement coverage notice — and it shapes how they experience the organization's values.
Extended family typically includes: grandchildren, aunts/uncles, cousins, in-laws not already listed. 3 days is common.
5. Parental Leave
△FMLA provides up to 12 weeks of unpaid leave for birth or adoption — it does not require paid leave. Paid parental leave is a voluntary benefit. The sector is shifting rapidly: organizations that cannot compete on salary increasingly offer parental leave as a differentiator. Sector average for primary caregiver: 8-12 weeks fully paid. Best practice: 12+ weeks.
A meaningful secondary caregiver benefit reduces inequity between parents and signals that the organization does not assume one parent is the default caregiver.
Best practice: apply the same benefit to birth, adoption, and foster care. Distinctions between how parents join their families create unequal and inequitable outcomes.
A return commitment is common for fully paid parental leave programs and is generally enforceable as a repayment agreement. If the employee does not return, the org may recoup a pro-rated portion of paid leave wages.
6. Personal Days
Personal days acknowledge that employees sometimes need time for life events that are not illness or vacation — appointments, religious observance, family obligations, mental health. They reduce friction and normalize human needs.
7. Unpaid Leave of Absence
Extended unpaid leave (30-90 days) is a valuable retention tool for employees facing life circumstances that fall outside FMLA. It costs the organization only continuation coverage and coverage for the role — and retains experienced staff who would otherwise resign.
8. Holidays & Other Paid Leave
Nonprofit sector standard: 10-13 paid holidays. Include federal holidays plus any organizational-specific days (e.g., day after Thanksgiving, week between Christmas and New Year).
Floating holidays allow employees to observe religious, cultural, or personal days that are not on the standard holiday calendar. They are an equity tool — a fixed holiday calendar built around Christian and U.S. federal holidays may not reflect the full diversity of a team.
For mission-driven organizations, paid volunteer time signals alignment between stated values and organizational practice. It also functions as a staff development and engagement benefit.

Track 12-week FMLA entitlement per employee in the rolling 12-month period. Required for compliance — employers must know how much FMLA each employee has used and how much remains.
ℹFMLA entitlement is calculated on a rolling 12-month basis measured backward from the date of any FMLA use. An employee who used 8 weeks six months ago and now requests more leave has only 4 weeks remaining — not a full 12. Track actual days/weeks used, not just current leave periods. Intermittent leave must be tracked to the day or hour.
