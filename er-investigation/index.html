Employee Relations Framework
Principles, applicable law, and structures for managing employee relations issues consistently and lawfully.
⚖Employee relations decisions carry legal risk. Every significant ER action — including disciplinary decisions, accommodation responses, leave approvals, and investigation outcomes — should be documented thoroughly and reviewed by HR or counsel before implementation. Inconsistent treatment across similarly situated employees is one of the most common bases for employment claims.
PrincipleApplication
ConsistencySimilarly situated employees must be treated similarly. Document the basis for any differential treatment. If two employees commit the same violation and receive different consequences, the difference must be documented and justified by a relevant, non-protected factor.
PromptnessComplaints and incidents must be addressed promptly. Delay signals tolerance. EEOC guidance requires prompt and appropriate corrective action once harassment or discrimination is known or should have been known.
ProportionalityThe response must fit the severity of the conduct. Progressive discipline is appropriate for performance and minor conduct issues. Serious misconduct may warrant immediate termination regardless of prior discipline history.
ConfidentialityInvestigations and complaints must be kept confidential to the extent possible. That is not absolute — investigators need to interview witnesses. Information should be shared on a need-to-know basis only. Blanket confidentiality orders to employees raise NLRA concerns.
Non-RetaliationRetaliation for reporting a complaint, cooperating in an investigation, or exercising a protected right is unlawful and often more legally dangerous than the underlying conduct. Document all post-complaint actions and scrutinize any adverse action against a complaining party.

Investigation Protocol
Step-by-step framework for conducting a workplace investigation. Apply proportionality — the process scales to the severity of the allegation.
△Not all complaints require a formal investigation. A complaint about a coworker's messy desk is different from a complaint about sexual harassment. The decision about whether and how to investigate should be proportional to the severity of the allegation and the legal exposure involved. When in doubt, investigate — the cost of under-investigating is almost always greater than the cost of over-investigating.
1
Receive and document the complaint
Record the complaint in writing immediately — date received, reporter, alleged conduct, alleged actor, dates of conduct, witnesses mentioned. Do not evaluate credibility at this stage. Your obligation to investigate is triggered by receipt, not by your belief that the complaint is credible.
2
Assess the need for immediate action
Determine whether the alleged actor needs to be placed on administrative leave or physically separated from the complainant. Err toward protecting the complainant. Administrative leave pending investigation is not discipline and should be communicated as such to the respondent.
3
Plan the investigation
Identify scope: what allegations are being investigated, what questions need to be answered, what evidence exists. Identify witnesses. Determine interview order — complainant first, witnesses next, respondent last. Document the investigation plan before you begin interviewing.
4
Conduct interviews
Interview the complainant first. Then key witnesses. Then the respondent. Each interview must be conducted privately. Take detailed contemporaneous notes. Do not share one party's statements with another during the investigation. After each interview, send a brief summary to the interviewee and ask them to confirm or correct it.
5
Gather and review documentary evidence
Emails, text messages, calendar entries, performance records, prior complaints, security footage, attendance records — whatever is relevant. Preserve everything. Once litigation is threatened or filed, a litigation hold is required immediately to prevent spoliation claims.
6
Analyze the evidence and make a finding
Apply the preponderance of the evidence standard — more likely than not. Document the finding: substantiated, unsubstantiated, or inconclusive. Base findings on the totality of evidence, not a single witness's credibility alone. Do not make credibility determinations based on demeanor.
7
Determine and implement corrective action
If substantiated, corrective action must be proportional and prompt. If unsubstantiated, document that and close. Communicate the outcome to the complainant — without sharing the respondent's specific consequences. The complainant has a right to know the complaint was taken seriously and addressed.
8
Document and close
Prepare a written investigation summary: complaint received, steps taken, evidence reviewed, finding, corrective action implemented. Retain all investigation documents in a confidential file separate from personnel files for the duration of employment plus a minimum of 3 years.

Witness Interview Framework
ℹEvery witness interview follows the same structure. Take detailed notes during the interview. Do not share one witness's statements with another during the investigation. After each interview, send a brief summary to the interviewee and ask them to confirm or correct it — this creates a contemporaneous record.
Opening Every Interview
"I am conducting a workplace investigation. I am interviewing several people. What we discuss should remain confidential — please do not discuss this with coworkers while the investigation is active. I will be taking notes. Do you have questions before we begin?"
Complainant Questions
Tell me what happened in your own words. / When and where did this occur? / Who else was present? / Has this happened before? / Did you report it to anyone? / How has this affected your work? / What outcome are you hoping for?
Respondent Questions
A concern has been raised and I am investigating. [Present allegation without naming complainant if possible.] Tell me your perspective. / Is there context I am missing? / Are there witnesses who could speak to this? / Is there anything else I should know?
Closing Every Interview
"Here is what I noted from our conversation: [summarize]. Is that accurate? Anything to add or correct? Keep this confidential. Come to me if you think of anything else or if you experience any retaliation."
