Pay Practices & Wage Compliance
Texas wage payment law, payroll setup documentation, garnishment handling, final pay requirements, and pay practice policies the organization must maintain.
Payroll Configuration
Core payroll settings that drive compliance obligations and employee communications.
Texas requires wages to be paid at least semi-monthly. Weekly and bi-weekly are also compliant.
Texas requires written employee authorization for mandatory direct deposit. Cannot require it without written consent.
The workweek start day determines overtime calculation for nonexempt employees. Must be consistent.
⚖Texas follows federal FLSA for overtime. There is no Texas state overtime law that exceeds the federal standard. Nonexempt employees must be paid 1.5x their regular rate for all hours over 40 in a workweek — not per day. The workweek is a fixed recurring period of 168 consecutive hours. An employer cannot average hours across two workweeks to avoid overtime liability.

Final Pay Requirements
Texas law governing the timing and content of final wages.
⚠Texas final pay requirements are strict and violations carry penalties. Late final pay exposes the organization to liability for the unpaid wages plus a penalty equal to the daily wage rate for each day the payment is late, up to the total amount owed.
Separation TypeFinal Pay DeadlineIncludes
Involuntary — Discharged / Laid OffWithin 6 calendar days of the discharge dateAll earned wages. Accrued PTO only if pay policy requires payout. Deductions for unreturned equipment are generally not permitted from final wages.
Voluntary — Employee ResignationNext regular payday following resignationAll earned wages through the last day worked. Same PTO rules apply.
Involuntary — Reduction in ForceWithin 6 calendar daysSame as discharge. Document the RIF as a business decision — separate from any performance concerns.
ℹTexas does not require employers to pay out accrued PTO at separation unless the written policy requires it. If the handbook or employment agreement states that accrued PTO is paid out upon separation, it is a wage and must be included in final pay. Review your PTO policy language carefully before a separation event.
Texas Payday Law — Employee Enforcement Rights
⚠Employees have the right to file a wage claim with the Texas Workforce Commission (TWC) if they believe final wages were not paid on time or in the correct amount. The TWC investigates and can order the employer to pay the wages owed plus administrative penalties. Employers who retaliate against an employee for filing a wage claim are subject to additional liability.
ElementDetail
Filing deadlineEmployee must file within 180 days of the date the wages were due. This deadline is strictly enforced by the TWC.
Where to fileTexas Workforce Commission, Labor Law Section. Online at twc.texas.gov. The process is administrative — does not require an attorney.
What the TWC can orderPayment of all wages owed, plus a penalty up to the full amount owed in some circumstances. The TWC can also refer cases with willful violations for criminal prosecution.
Employer responseEmployers receive notice of the claim and must respond within the TWC's prescribed timeframe. Failing to respond results in a default order against the employer.
Retaliation prohibitionAn employer cannot terminate, reduce pay, or otherwise retaliate against an employee for filing a wage claim. Retaliation is a separate violation subject to additional penalties.
Regular Rate of Pay
The foundation of accurate overtime calculation. Most nonprofit overtime errors originate here.
⚖The "regular rate of pay" for overtime purposes is not the same as the hourly rate. It is a calculated rate that must include all remuneration for employment paid to or on behalf of the employee — with only specific exclusions permitted by statute. Using the base hourly rate alone when calculating overtime for employees who receive bonuses, shift differentials, or other compensation is a wage violation under the FLSA.
What Must Be Included in the Regular Rate
Compensation TypeIncluded in Regular Rate?How to Handle
Base hourly wagesYes — alwaysDivide total base wages by total hours in the workweek
Non-discretionary bonuses (production, attendance, performance tied to metrics)Yes — must be includedProrate the bonus across the weeks in the bonus period; recalculate OT rate for those weeks
Shift differentials (evening, weekend premium)YesAdd the differential to total straight-time pay; divide by total hours to get the regular rate
On-call payYes, if employee is required to remain on premises or use is so restricted as to constitute hours workedDetermine whether on-call time is "hours worked" first
Discretionary bonuses (genuinely given at employer's sole discretion, not tied to any formula or promise)No — excludedMust be truly discretionary — not based on any pre-announced criteria or formula
Employer-paid health insurance premiumsNo — excludedExcluded under FLSA § 207(e)(4)
Employer 401(k)/403(b) contributionsNo — excludedExcluded under FLSA § 207(e)(4)
Overtime premium payments themselvesNo — excludedExcluded to avoid circular calculation
Expense reimbursements (actual expenses)No — excludedMust be actual and reasonably approximate actual expense
Calculation Example
Scenario: Nonexempt employee works 48 hours in a workweek at $18/hour base rate and receives a $200 non-discretionary bonus for meeting a program target that week.

Step 1 — Total straight-time compensation: (48 hrs × $18) + $200 bonus = $864 + $200 = $1,064
Step 2 — Regular rate of pay: $1,064 ÷ 48 hours = $22.17/hour
Step 3 — Overtime premium: 8 OT hours × ($22.17 × 0.5 half-time premium) = $88.67
Step 4 — Total pay due: $1,064 (straight-time already paid) + $88.67 (OT premium) = $1,152.67

If the employer had simply used the $18 base rate: $18 × 1.5 × 8 = $216 OT — underpaying by $24.67 for that week alone. Across multiple employees over months, this compounds into significant wage liability.
△Organizations that pay any form of non-discretionary bonus, differential, or additional compensation to nonexempt employees should audit their overtime calculations annually. FLSA statute of limitations is 2 years (3 for willful violations). The DOL Wage and Hour Division can recover back wages for the full period. Nonprofit employers are not exempt from FLSA.
Deductions, Advances & Off-Cycle Payroll
What employers can and cannot deduct. When and how to process payments outside the regular pay cycle.
Authorized Deductions
ℹTexas follows federal law on deductions with important state-specific protections. An employer generally cannot deduct from wages without prior written authorization from the employee — except for items specifically permitted by law. Unauthorized deductions from wages are a Texas Payday Law violation.

Pay Advances
ElementRequirement
Written advance agreementRequired before issuing any pay advance. Must specify the amount advanced, the repayment schedule, and the deduction amount per pay period.
Minimum wage floorRepayment deductions cannot reduce the employee's net pay below federal or state minimum wage in any workweek where hours are worked.
SeparationIf an employee separates before the advance is fully repaid, the remaining balance can be deducted from final pay only if the written advance agreement authorizes it. The deduction still cannot reduce pay below minimum wage for the final pay period.
InterestTexas does not prohibit employers from charging interest on advances, but the agreement must be in writing and the terms disclosed before the advance is issued.

FLSA Exemption Tests
To be exempt from overtime, an employee must meet both the salary basis test AND a duties test. Failing either test means the employee is nonexempt and overtime-eligible regardless of job title.
⚠Job titles do not determine exemption status. An employee called "Manager" or "Director" who does not meet both the salary and duties tests is nonexempt and must be paid overtime. Misclassification is one of the most common and costly FLSA violations for nonprofit employers — and it is audited regularly by the DOL Wage and Hour Division.
Salary Basis Test — Required for All White-Collar Exemptions
ElementRequirementCurrent Threshold
Salary basisEmployee must receive a predetermined fixed salary that is not subject to reduction based on quality or quantity of work. Deductions from salary that violate the salary basis rule destroy the exemption for the affected period.N/A — this is a qualitative test
Minimum salary levelEmployee must earn at least the FLSA salary threshold per week. Texas has no higher state threshold.$684 / week ($35,568 annualized)
2024 DOL rule (vacated)A 2024 DOL rule raised the threshold to $1,128/week — this rule was vacated by a federal court in November 2024. The $684/week threshold is the operative standard as of 2025. Monitor for further rulemaking.$684/week — confirm current status with counsel
Highly compensated employees (HCE)Employees earning $107,432 or more annually (with at least $684/week on a salary or fee basis) who customarily perform exempt duties qualify under a relaxed duties test.$107,432 / year
White-Collar Duties Tests
ExemptionPrimary Duty RequirementNonprofit Relevance
ExecutivePrimary duty is management of the enterprise or a recognized department. Customarily directs 2+ employees. Has authority to hire or fire — or whose recommendations carry particular weight.Applies to EDs, VPs, Directors who genuinely manage staff. Does not apply to supervisors who primarily do individual work.
AdministrativePrimary duty is non-manual work directly related to management or general business operations. Includes the exercise of discretion and independent judgment with respect to matters of significance.The most commonly misapplied exemption. Office work is not enough — the work must involve actual discretion on matters of consequence, not just following established procedures.
Professional — LearnedPrimary duty requires advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning customarily acquired by a prolonged course of specialized intellectual instruction.Licensed attorneys, CPAs, engineers, architects, physicians. A degree requirement alone does not satisfy this — the work itself must require the advanced knowledge.
Professional — CreativePrimary duty requires invention, imagination, originality, or talent in a recognized field of artistic or creative endeavor.Narrow. Graphic designers, writers, musicians — only if the work involves genuine creative latitude, not production work.
Computer EmployeeSystem analysts, programmers, software engineers — with specific duties requirements. Must be paid $684/week on salary OR $27.63/hour.Applicable to in-house tech staff. Does not apply to help desk, data entry, or general IT support roles.
Common Misclassification Patterns in Nonprofits
Role TypeMisclassification RiskCorrect Analysis
Program CoordinatorsHigh — often titled and paid as exempt but primarily execute established proceduresIf the role follows standard operating procedures and escalates decisions upward, it likely fails the administrative duties test. Review whether discretion is genuine.
Case Managers / Client ServicesHigh — social work and client-facing roles frequently misclassifiedCase managers who follow prescribed care plans with limited independent authority are typically nonexempt. Licensure alone does not create the learned professional exemption.
Development / Fundraising AssociatesMedium — depends on actual duties and decision-making authorityIf the role primarily executes campaigns under direction with limited discretion, likely nonexempt. Senior fundraisers with authority over strategy and donor relationships may qualify.
Communications / Marketing StaffMedium — creative exemption is narrowCreative exemption requires genuine artistic discretion — not execution of brand standards. Most communications roles in nonprofits are administrative or nonexempt.
△When in doubt about exemption status, classify as nonexempt. The cost of tracking hours and paying overtime is far less than the cost of an FLSA misclassification investigation. Audits can recover back wages for up to 3 years (willful violations). Self-auditing exemption classifications annually is a best practice the DOL explicitly recommends.
Payroll Error Correction
Underpayments, overpayments, and missed payroll runs. Every error has a protocol — the worst response is to wait.
⚠Payroll errors — in either direction — are wage compliance events, not administrative inconveniences. Underpayments may violate the FLSA and Texas Payday Law. Overpayment recovery requires specific handling. Both require prompt action, written documentation, and employee communication.
Underpayment Corrections
SituationRequired ActionTiming
Employee was paid less than wages earnedIssue a corrective payment for the full underpaid amount. Do not defer to the next regular pay cycle if that would leave the employee underpaid for more than one pay period.As soon as discovered — typically within 1–2 business days
Overtime was calculated incorrectlyCalculate the correct regular rate and overtime owed. Issue a corrective payment for the difference. If the error recurred across multiple pay periods, calculate the total liability and correct all periods.As soon as discovered. If systemic, complete full audit before correcting to avoid multiple piecemeal payments.
Missed payroll run entirelyProcess an off-cycle payroll immediately. Notify affected employees the same day the error is discovered — do not wait for them to ask. Document the cause and corrective action taken.Same day discovery — next business day at absolute latest
New hire not entered in payrollProcess a corrective payment for all unpaid periods. This may also trigger a Texas new hire reporting failure — verify and correct if needed.Immediately upon discovery
Overpayment Recovery
ℹRecovering an overpayment requires written notice to the employee and, in most cases, written authorization before any deduction is taken from future wages. Texas does not permit unilateral deductions from future wages without written employee consent — except in the same pay period the overpayment occurred.
Recovery MethodRequirementsLimits
Same-period reversalIf discovered in the same pay period, the amount can be corrected before the payroll run processes without additional authorization.Must occur before wages are actually paid to the employee
Written repayment agreementNotify the employee in writing of the overpayment and proposed recovery schedule. Obtain written authorization before taking any deduction from future pay. The agreement must specify the total amount, the per-period deduction, and the recovery period.Deductions cannot reduce net pay below minimum wage in any workweek. Recovery spread across multiple periods is permissible and often preferable.
Voluntary repayment by employeeEmployee may choose to repay the full amount directly. Document with a signed receipt and retain in the payroll record.Cannot be coerced. Cannot condition continued employment on immediate lump-sum repayment.
Post-separation recoveryIf the employee has separated, the remaining balance can be deducted from final pay only if a prior written overpayment agreement authorizes it and it does not reduce final pay below minimum wage. If no agreement exists, recovery must be through demand letter or civil action.No self-help deductions from final pay without prior written authorization
Documentation Requirements
For Every Error CorrectionContent Required
Error memo (internal)Date the error was discovered, nature of the error, affected employees, pay periods involved, total dollar amount, cause of the error, and corrective action taken. Retain in payroll records.
Employee notificationWritten notice to the affected employee explaining the error, the correction, and — for overpayments — the proposed recovery plan. Retain signed acknowledgment.
Payroll record amendmentUpdate payroll records to reflect the corrected amounts. If using a payroll provider, ensure the correction is reflected in the provider's system and the tax filings for the affected period are accurate.
W-2 corrections (if applicable)If the error crosses a calendar year boundary or affects reported wages, a W-2c (corrected W-2) may be required. Consult your payroll provider or CPA before year-end.
⚖An employee who files a TWC wage claim for an underpayment that was not promptly corrected faces a stronger case because the delay is evidence. Correcting errors promptly and with documentation is both the legal obligation and the organizational interest. If a systemic error is discovered — affecting multiple employees over multiple periods — consult employment counsel before issuing corrections, as the DOL and TWC have different audit-trigger thresholds for self-reported violations versus complaints.
