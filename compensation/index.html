Market Position Strategy
The single most consequential compensation decision the organization makes. This anchors every pay band in the system.
ℹ Market position determines where this organization targets its pay relative to the broader market for comparable roles. A 50th percentile position means half of comparable organizations pay more, half pay less. There is no universally correct answer — the right position depends on budget, talent strategy, and mission alignment. The default recommendation for most early-stage nonprofits and social enterprises is the 50th percentile.
25th
Percentile
Pays below the market midpoint. Sustainable only with a strong non-monetary value proposition. Higher voluntary turnover risk. Common in very early-stage or underfunded organizations.
50th
Percentile · Recommended
Pays at the market midpoint. Competitive for mission-aligned talent. Defensible to boards and funders. The standard for well-run nonprofits with established infrastructure.
75th
Percentile
Pays above the market midpoint. Strong talent attraction and retention tool. Appropriate for roles where talent scarcity is high or the organization competes with the private sector.
90th
Percentile
Pays at the top of the market. Signals compensation as a strategic priority. Requires very strong budget capacity and board alignment. Typically reserved for key executive roles.

Geographic Cost-of-Labor Adjustment
Location materially affects market pay. These multipliers are applied to national benchmark data to produce location-adjusted ranges. Source: BLS Occupational Employment Statistics, ERI Geographic Assessor methodology.
ℹ Geographic differentials reflect the cost of labor, not cost of living. The distinction matters — a role that commands $65,000 in the Dallas metro commands approximately $85,000–$92,000 in Washington DC not because DC is expensive to live in, but because the labor market for that role in DC is more competitive. These multipliers are calibrated for nonprofit and social impact roles specifically.

Methodology
How these numbers are built

Market reference ranges are derived from nonprofit and social impact compensation benchmarks including Candid/GuideStar Nonprofit Compensation Report, BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics, SHRM Compensation Benchmarking data, and ERI Salary Assessor regional differentials.

All ranges reflect knowledge cutoff data and should be verified against current survey sources for high-stakes decisions. Override fields are provided for every benchmark.

This tool supports compensation decision-making — it does not replace the judgment of a qualified internal compensation professional or general counsel for executive compensation matters.

Compa-Ratio Analysis
Where each employee's actual salary sits relative to the midpoint of their pay band. A compa-ratio of 1.00 means they are paid exactly at the market midpoint.
ℹ Compa-ratio = Actual Salary ÷ Band Midpoint. A ratio below 0.85 indicates the employee may be significantly underpaid relative to market. A ratio above 1.15 indicates they may be above the band and should be reviewed. New hires typically start between 0.85–0.95. Tenured high performers typically fall between 1.00–1.10.

Internal Equity Analysis
Identifies pay inconsistencies across roles at the same level or within the same function. Compression, inversion, and unexplained variance are flagged for review.
ℹ Internal equity requires that employees in similar roles with similar scope, experience, and performance receive comparable pay. Unexplained differentials — particularly those that correlate with protected class characteristics — represent significant legal and organizational risk. This analysis identifies structural gaps; investigation of individual differentials requires HR and counsel judgment.
Complete the Compa-Ratio tab with actual salaries to generate the equity analysis.
Merit Increase Matrix
Cross-references performance rating with compa-ratio to recommend merit increase ranges for each employee. The standard tool for managing merit budgets consistently and defensibly.
ℹThe merit matrix is applied at the annual review cycle to translate performance ratings into consistent pay adjustments. It prevents two outcomes: managers rewarding low performers too generously and managers under-rewarding high performers who are already paid above midpoint. The correct increase for any employee depends on both their performance AND where they sit in the band. Employees below midpoint who perform well should receive larger increases — both to reward performance and to bring pay to market.
Total merit pool as a % of total payroll. Sector average: 3–4%.
How to Use This Matrix
Find the employee's compa-ratio
From the Compa-Ratio tab. Below 0.90 means they are underpaid vs. market midpoint. Above 1.10 means they are above midpoint.
Find their performance rating
From the completed performance review. Rating must be documented before merit decisions are made — not retroactively assigned.
Apply the matrix range
The intersection gives the recommended increase range. Supervisor may position within the range based on additional context, with HR documentation.
Promotion & Reclassification Framework
Salary adjustment standards when an employee moves to a higher track, level, or role. Ensures equity and consistency — and protects the organization from ad hoc decisions that create compression.
△Promotion salary decisions made without a framework become the source of your next compression problem. When managers negotiate raises individually at promotion time, the result is a pay structure built on relationship and advocacy rather than equity. A documented framework does not limit manager flexibility — it creates a defensible floor that protects everyone.

Budget Impact Model
Projects total compensation cost against operating budget. Flags when payroll as a percentage of budget exceeds or falls outside sustainable range.
Enter the organization's total annual operating budget including all revenue sources.
Typical range: 18–32% of base salary. Includes employer FICA (7.65%), health insurance, retirement match, and other benefits. Use 25% as a default if unknown.
Industry standard for nonprofits: 60–80% of operating budget. Below 55% may indicate underinvestment in people. Above 80% creates financial fragility.

Executive Compensation
Complete compensation package modeling for executive-track roles. Generates a board-facing memo with structured comparability data. This memo is presented by the advisor — never by the executive whose compensation is being reviewed.
⚖ For 501(c)(3) organizations, executive compensation must be reasonable and documented through the rebuttable presumption process under IRC § 4958: (1) approved by an authorized body free of conflict of interest, (2) based on comparability data from similarly situated organizations, and (3) adequately documented contemporaneously. This tool supports step 2. Steps 1 and 3 require board action and legal counsel. The IRS has increased scrutiny of executive compensation — document every data point.
